Q: What is hantavirus?
A: Hantavirus refers to a family of viruses primarily spread by rodents that can cause serious illness in humans, including Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS) and Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome (HFRS).

Q: How do humans get hantavirus?
A: Humans are typically infected by inhaling aerosolized particles contaminated with infected rodent urine, droppings, or saliva. Infection may also occur through rodent bites or direct contact with contaminated surfaces.

Q: Can hantavirus spread from person to person?
A: Most hantavirus strains are not believed to spread efficiently between humans. However, Andes virus in South America has demonstrated limited person-to-person transmission during prolonged close contact.

Q: What are the early symptoms of hantavirus?
A: Early symptoms commonly include fever, fatigue, muscle aches, headaches, dizziness, nausea, chills, and abdominal discomfort.

Q: What are severe hantavirus symptoms?
A: Severe cases may progress to coughing, fluid buildup in the lungs, severe shortness of breath, respiratory distress, kidney complications, and potentially respiratory failure.

Q: What is Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome?
A: HPS is a severe respiratory illness caused by certain hantavirus strains in the Americas. It can rapidly progress to pulmonary edema and respiratory failure.

Q: What is Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome?
A: HFRS is a hantavirus-associated disease more common in Europe and Asia that primarily affects the kidneys and circulatory system.

Q: How deadly is hantavirus?
A: Mortality rates vary depending on the strain and severity of infection. Severe HPS cases can have significant fatality rates without rapid medical intervention.

Q: Is hantavirus airborne?
A: Hantavirus is not considered airborne in the same way as viruses like measles or COVID-19. However, infection can occur through inhalation of aerosolized particles contaminated by infected rodent waste.

Q: Can pets spread hantavirus?
A: Household pets are not considered primary carriers of hantavirus, although they may bring infected rodents into human environments.

Q: How can hantavirus infection be prevented?
A: Prevention focuses on rodent control, sealing entry points, avoiding contact with rodent waste, ventilating enclosed spaces, and using safe cleanup procedures with disinfectants and protective equipment.

Q: Is there a cure for hantavirus?
A: There is currently no universally approved antiviral cure for hantavirus infection. Treatment primarily involves supportive medical care and intensive monitoring.

Q: What rodents carry hantavirus?
A: Rodent carriers vary by region and strain but may include deer mice, cotton rats, rice rats, bank voles, and other wild rodent species.

Q: Where is hantavirus found?
A: Hantavirus infections have been reported across North America, South America, Europe, and Asia.

Q: How long is the incubation period for hantavirus?
A: The incubation period is generally estimated to range from one to eight weeks following exposure.

Q: Can cleaning rodent droppings cause hantavirus infection?
A: Yes. Sweeping or vacuuming rodent droppings may aerosolize contaminated particles and increase infection risk if proper cleaning procedures are not followed.

Q: What should someone do if they suspect hantavirus exposure?
A: Individuals with suspected exposure or symptoms should seek medical attention promptly, especially if respiratory symptoms develop after rodent exposure.
