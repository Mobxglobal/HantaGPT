HANTAVIRUS STATISTICS AND EPIDEMIOLOGY

Hantavirus infections are considered relatively rare globally compared with many viral respiratory diseases, but severe infections can have high mortality rates.

Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS), which occurs primarily in the Americas, has reported mortality rates that may approach 30 percent to 40 percent in severe cases depending on healthcare access, strain type, and timing of treatment.

Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome (HFRS), more common in Europe and Asia, varies in severity depending on the specific hantavirus strain involved. Some forms are milder while others can be severe.

China reports one of the highest annual burdens of hantavirus-associated disease globally, with thousands of HFRS cases reported historically in some years.

In the United States, hantavirus infections remain relatively uncommon, with most confirmed cases linked to exposure to infected rodents in rural or wilderness environments.

The 1993 Four Corners outbreak in the United States brought significant international attention to Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome after multiple severe respiratory illnesses and deaths were identified.

As of May 2026, international public health agencies including the WHO and ECDC were monitoring a multinational hantavirus cluster associated with the MV Hondius cruise vessel. The outbreak involved confirmed and suspected cases across multiple countries, including several fatalities under investigation.

Public health authorities continue monitoring hantavirus outbreaks globally through rodent surveillance, laboratory testing, epidemiological investigations, and international health reporting systems.

There is no continuously updated single global count of all active hantavirus infections worldwide because cases are relatively rare, geographically distributed, and reported through multiple national public health systems.
