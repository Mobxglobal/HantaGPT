The 1993 Four Corners outbreak in the southwestern United States was one of the most significant hantavirus events in North American history. The outbreak occurred in the Four Corners region where Arizona, New Mexico, Colorado, and Utah meet. Investigators identified Sin Nombre virus carried by deer mice as the primary cause of severe respiratory illness cases later classified as Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome.

The Four Corners outbreak led to major advances in hantavirus surveillance, rodent ecology research, and public health awareness in the United States. Environmental conditions associated with increased rodent populations were believed to contribute to the outbreak.

Argentina has experienced multiple Andes virus outbreaks associated with Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome. Some outbreaks raised concern because Andes virus demonstrated limited person-to-person transmission under prolonged close-contact conditions.

Chile has also reported recurring Andes virus infections and HPS outbreaks, particularly in rural regions with exposure to infected rodents and contaminated environments.

South Korea has historically experienced cases of Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome associated with Hantaan virus. The disease gained international attention during and after the Korean War due to infections among military personnel.

China reports a significant number of Hemorrhagic Fever with Renal Syndrome cases annually compared with many other countries. Multiple hantavirus strains circulate within rodent populations in different regions of China.

European countries including Finland, Sweden, Germany, and parts of the Balkans have reported hantavirus infections associated with strains such as Puumala virus and Dobrava-Belgrade virus.

In 2017, outbreaks of Seoul virus linked to domesticated rat populations were investigated in the United States and the United Kingdom. Public health agencies traced infections to contact with infected pet rats and breeding facilities.

Panama has reported hantavirus outbreaks associated with Choclo virus, particularly in agricultural regions with rodent exposure risks.

Public health authorities worldwide continue monitoring hantavirus outbreaks through surveillance programs, rodent population studies, laboratory testing, and epidemiological investigations.
